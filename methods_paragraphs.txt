BAYES' THEOREM — PREVALENCE-ADJUSTED PPV FOR OUR COHORT
================================================================================

Our cohort ARDS prevalence: P = 2,419 / 19,052 = 12.7%

Formula:
  PPV_adjusted = (Se x P) / (Se x P + (1 - Sp) x (1 - P))

Inputs:
  Se = 0.52  (validation cohort)
  Sp = 0.69  (validation cohort)
  P  = 0.127 (our cohort)

Calculation:
  Numerator   = 0.52 x 0.127                     = 0.0660
  Denominator = 0.52 x 0.127 + 0.31 x 0.873      = 0.0660 + 0.2706 = 0.3366
  PPV_adj     = 0.0660 / 0.3366                  = 19.6% ~ 20%

Verification at validation-cohort prevalence (53%):
  (0.52 x 0.53) / (0.52 x 0.53 + 0.31 x 0.47) = 0.2756 / 0.4213 = 65.4% ~ 66% [CHECKS OUT]

IMPORTANT CAVEAT: Hochberg et al. explicitly note that Se and Sp are secondary outcomes
and likely underestimated, because the clinical adjudication reference did not require
sustained hypoxemia — some classifier-negative patients may have had true ARDS that
resolved before the 12-24h persistence window. The paper states: "If true negatives are
underestimated, as we expect, sensitivity is also likely underestimated." The ~20%
adjusted PPV is therefore a lower bound; the true value is likely modestly higher.

DRAFT LIMITATIONS PARAGRAPH FOR CLAIRE
================================================================================

The persistent AHRF cohort was identified using an EHR-based classifier validated by
Hochberg et al. (Crit Care Med 2026;54:654-660). In the independent 90-patient validation
cohort — drawn from a geographically distinct academic medical ICU with 53% ARDS
prevalence — the classifier's positive predictive value (PPV) was 66% (95% CI 50-81%).
However, PPV is prevalence-dependent. The classifier's sensitivity and specificity in the
validation cohort were 52% (95% CI 38-67%) and 69% (95% CI 55-82%), respectively.
Applying Bayes' theorem, the expected PPV in our cohort — where the base rate of
persistent moderate-to-severe ARDS among mechanically ventilated patients is substantially
lower (~12.7%; 2,419 of 19,052 MV encounters) — is approximately 20%. This means a
meaningful proportion of patients in the persistent AHRF cohort may not have met full
Berlin ARDS criteria at the time of their index hypoxemia. Hochberg et al. note that
classifier sensitivity is likely underestimated because the clinical adjudication reference
standard did not require sustained hypoxemia; accordingly, the 20% estimate should be
interpreted as approximate. Critically, misclassification of individual ARDS status does
not bias the primary analysis, which estimates attending-level variation in tidal-volume
practice across all patients meeting hypoxemia criteria — a population enriched for but
not restricted to Berlin-definition ARDS.

The 66% PPV above refers solely to the ARDS classifier performance and should not be
conflated with two separate validation figures: the 93% agreement reported for the
attending-assignment algorithm (chart-review validation; [citation]) and the 96%
recent-year accuracy of that same algorithm ([citation]).

DRAFT METHODS PARAGRAPH FOR CLAIRE
================================================================================

COVID-19 status was defined as a binary indicator at the hospitalization level.
A hospitalization was classified as COVID-19–positive if a positive SARS-CoV-2
nucleic acid amplification or antigen test was recorded in the microbiology
record within 14 days before or on the date of first intubation for that
encounter. All hospitalizations without a matching positive test result within
this window were classified as COVID-19–negative (reference category). This
binary indicator was held constant across all patient-ventilator-days within a
hospitalization and was included as a patient-level covariate in all primary
mixed-effects logistic regression models to adjust for systematic differences in
LTVV adherence associated with active SARS-CoV-2 infection.

DRAFT METHODS/LIMITATIONS PARAGRAPH FOR CLAIRE
================================================================================

To ensure stable estimation of provider-level random effects, we restricted the
analysis to attending physicians who were assigned as the attending of record for
at least 25 invasive mechanical ventilation encounters (day 1) during the study
period. Of 1,580 unique providers who supervised at least one qualifying
mechanical ventilation encounter with a non-null provider identifier, 176 (11.1%)
met this threshold and were included in the analysis; 1,404 (88.9%) were
excluded. The majority of excluded providers had very limited ICU exposure over
the 14-year study period: excluded providers supervised a median of 2 [IQR 1–4]
day-1 encounters, and 80.5% supervised fewer than five total mechanical
ventilation encounters. Nearly half (49.6%) supervised only a single encounter.
This pattern is consistent with occasional on-call coverage, rotating trainees,
or providers whose primary clinical practice was outside the ICU setting.
Providers with fewer than 25 encounters contribute insufficient data for stable
random-effect estimation; including them would increase residual variance in the
provider random intercept without meaningfully improving representativeness. This
threshold is consistent with prior multilevel analyses of provider-level variation
in critical care practice.

================================================================================
DRAFT METHODS PARAGRAPH FOR CLAIRE
================================================================================

Patient-ventilator-days during which extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO)
was active were excluded from the analytic dataset, as tidal-volume targets are
not clinically applicable during ECMO support. ECMO activity was identified from
the clif_ecmo_mcs table (mcs_group = 'ecmo') and flagged at the calendar-date
level; any patient-day on which ECMO was recorded was removed from the primary
analytic file. This exclusion affected 3,300 patient-days (3.2% of the 101,697 pre-exclusion
patient-day total in the persistent AHRF cohort) across 704 unique hospitalizations. Encounters in which ECMO
was initiated at or before the time a patient first met persistent AHRF enrollment
criteria were additionally excluded at the cohort-eligibility stage (n = 294 encounters,
0.54% of 54,159 reviewed); these encounters never entered the analytic dataset.

TASK 11 — Confirm Outside-IMV-Transfer Handling
Completed: 2026-06-12
Source: Code review of ltvv_wrangler.ipynb (cells 10, 11, 23)
================================================================================

CONFIRMED HANDLING LOGIC
================================================================================

Outside-IMV-transfer patients (patients intubated at an outside hospital before
transfer to a study hospital) are NOT explicitly excluded from the cohort. There
is no admission_type, transfer-origin, or outside-hospital filter anywhere in the
pipeline. The hospitalization table is queried only for age_at_admission,
patient_id, and Elixhauser comorbidities (cell 23); transfer metadata is never
accessed.

INTUBATION TIME PROXY
intubation_dttm is defined as MIN(recorded_dttm) from the respiratory_support
table filtered to device_category ILIKE '%imv%' within episode 1 (cell 11,
intubation_times CTE). Code comment: "First IMV record per cohort hospitalization
within episode 1 = intubation proxy."

For patients transferred already intubated, this timestamp is the first time
ventilator settings were charted at the receiving hospital — not the actual
intubation time at the transferring facility. The pipeline has no mechanism to
detect or correct this.

DAY-1 ASCERTAINMENT WINDOW
The ≥5h window is applied from this EHR-based proxy:
  day1_recs: recorded_dttm >= intubation_dttm + INTERVAL 5 HOURS  (cell 11)

Example: patient intubated at outside hospital at 08:00, first charted locally
at 12:00 → intubation_dttm = 12:00 → ≥5h window begins at 17:00, nine hours
after true intubation. Any Vt measurement between 13:00 and 16:59 is excluded
from day-1. If no qualifying Vt exists after 17:00, the patient contributes no
day-1 observation.

COHORT ENTRY (cell 10)
cohort_meta uses vent_episode_id = '1' and vent_episode_duration_hours >= 24
from the CLIF hourly waterfall table. For outside-IMV-transfer patients, both
vent_episode_id and its duration count from the first local EHR IMV record —
the same proxy. These patients enter the cohort if their local-EHR IMV episode
is ≥24 hours in duration.

NULL INTUBATION TIME
Not applicable in practice: any patient who enters cohort_meta (requiring ≥1
IMV record in the hourly table) will always yield a non-null intubation_dttm
via MIN(). No recovery step is needed.

DIRECTION OF BIAS (day-1 stratum only)
For outside-IMV-transfer patients, the ≥5h window starts later than the true
≥5h mark. The measured Vt therefore reflects a later, more stabilized point in
the ventilation course. If these patients have already had settings titrated
during transport, day-1 adherence may be slightly overestimated for this
subgroup. The subsequent-day stratum is unaffected (no intubation-time reference).

================================================================================
DRAFT METHODS/LIMITATIONS PARAGRAPH FOR CLAIRE
================================================================================

The date and time of intubation were defined as the timestamp of the first
invasive mechanical ventilation (IMV) record in the local electronic health
record (EHR) for each hospitalization. For patients intubated at the study
hospital, this timestamp directly reflects the start of mechanical ventilation.
Patients transferred from outside hospitals while already receiving invasive
mechanical ventilation were not explicitly excluded from the cohort; for these
patients, the intubation proxy corresponds to the first time ventilator settings
were documented at the receiving hospital rather than the time of the original
intubation at the transferring facility. The day-1 measurement window (first
non-null tidal volume set ≥5 hours after the local intubation proxy) therefore
starts from an EHR-based surrogate that may underestimate elapsed time on the
ventilator for outside-transfer patients.

This limitation affects the day-1 stratum disproportionately. For
outside-IMV-transfer patients, the true elapsed ventilation time at the ≥5h
measurement point may be substantially longer than five hours, meaning the
measured tidal volume reflects a later and potentially more stabilized point in
the ventilation course. To the extent that outside-transfer patients have already
had ventilator settings titrated during transport, their day-1 adherence may be
slightly overestimated relative to patients intubated de novo at the study
hospital. The magnitude of this bias depends on the proportion of
outside-transfer patients in the cohort and the typical lag between true
intubation and first local EHR charting; these quantities are not directly
observable from the available data structure.
